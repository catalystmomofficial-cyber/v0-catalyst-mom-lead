# The BeHuman pattern catalog

The complete field guide, adapted from Wikipedia's "Signs of AI writing" (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing), a page maintained by the editors of WikiProject AI Cleanup after reviewing thousands of AI-generated drafts. Their framing matters: these are density signals, not proof. Humans do every one of these things occasionally. AI does them constantly, together, by default. Your job is to remove the defaults.

Read the section you need. Each pattern has three parts: what it looks like, why it reads as AI, and how to fix it.

---

## Part 1: Language and tone

### 1.1 Inflated significance (puffery)

**Looks like:** Ordinary facts dressed up as milestones. "The bakery stands as a testament to the town's enduring spirit." "The update marks a pivotal moment in the company's journey." "His work left an indelible mark on the field." Common carriers: stands as, serves as, plays a vital role, watershed moment, key turning point, deeply rooted, continues to captivate, enduring legacy, cementing its place.

**Why it reads as AI:** Models puff up whatever subject they are given because agreeable importance is the statistically safe output. The same sentence could be pasted onto a bakery, a battery factory, or a Baroque composer. Text that fits everything describes nothing.

**Fix:** Replace the claim of importance with the evidence of importance. "The bakery has been family-run since 1962 and still uses the original oven" beats any testament to any spirit. If there is no evidence, the sentence had no job. Delete it.

### 1.2 Promotional peacock language

**Looks like:** "Nestled in the heart of the city," "breathtaking views," "a must-visit destination," "rich cultural heritage," "vibrant community," "boasts state-of-the-art facilities."

**Why it reads as AI:** This is travel-brochure varnish applied indiscriminately, even to topics where nobody asked for enthusiasm. It signals that no writer stood behind the words.

**Fix:** Report, don't advertise. What is actually there? How big, how old, how many, compared to what? If you are writing actual marketing copy, sell with specifics and proof (numbers, named outcomes, real quotes), not varnish.

### 1.3 Editorializing and fake analysis

**Looks like:** "It's important to note that...", "It is worth remembering...", "No discussion of X would be complete without...", plus unearned interpretation slipped into neutral text.

**Why it reads as AI:** The model is narrating its own essay instead of delivering information. Telling the reader what to find important is a substitute for making it important.

**Fix:** If it is important, it will be obvious from the writing. State the fact and let it carry its own weight.

### 1.4 Significance tails (the ", -ing" clause)

**Looks like:** A normal sentence, then a comma, then a present-participle clause that assigns meaning: "The library added 40 new laptops, ensuring every student can access digital resources." "She won the regional title, highlighting the rise of chess in the district." Frequent tails: ensuring, highlighting, underscoring, reflecting, showcasing, emphasizing, demonstrating, solidifying, cementing.

**Why it reads as AI:** It welds an unverified conclusion onto a verified fact so the two travel together. It is the single most reliable AI tell in informative writing.

**Fix:** Cut the tail. If the conclusion is real and matters, give it its own sentence with its own support. Usually it evaporates on contact.

### 1.5 Negative parallelism

**Looks like:** "It's not just a phone, it's a lifestyle." "This isn't about speed. It's about trust." Also the triple-negative flourish: "No fluff. No filler. Just results."

**Why it reads as AI:** Manufactured profundity. The construction fakes a reveal without revealing anything, and models reach for it every time they want to sound insightful.

**Fix:** State the actual point directly. If the contrast is real, show both sides with specifics instead of a slogan.

### 1.6 Rule-of-three padding

**Looks like:** Every description stacks exactly three adjectives ("fast, flexible, and reliable"), every list has exactly three bullets, every argument has exactly three reasons.

**Why it reads as AI:** Three is the rhythm of least resistance, so models pad thin content or trim rich content to hit it. Once you notice the drumbeat you cannot unhear it.

**Fix:** Match the count to reality. Two items, five items, one item. If an adjective was added for rhythm rather than truth, it goes.

### 1.7 False ranges

**Looks like:** "From intimate gatherings to global movements." "From technical expertise to creative vision." "Everyone from students to CEOs."

**Why it reads as AI:** The structure implies a measured spectrum, but the endpoints are just two loosely related things dressed up to sound comprehensive.

**Fix:** Name who or what you actually mean. If there genuinely is a range, give its real ends with real units.

### 1.8 Vague attribution and weasel wording

**Looks like:** "Experts say," "some critics argue," "many believe," "industry reports suggest," "is widely regarded as," with no named source anywhere.

**Why it reads as AI:** Models generate the shape of a citation without the substance of one, because the training data is full of claims and thin on receipts.

**Fix:** Name the person, publication, or study, with a date if possible. If no source exists, the claim is an opinion; either own it explicitly or drop it.

### 1.9 Overused transitions

**Looks like:** Moreover, furthermore, additionally, consequently, notably, in addition, on the other hand, that being said, at the end of the day, deployed at the head of most sentences.

**Why it reads as AI:** They are joints installed where none were needed. Prose whose every sentence begins with a connective reads like it is being assembled, not written.

**Fix:** Delete the transition and read the two sentences. Nine times out of ten they connect fine on their own. Order your ideas so they flow; do not caulk the gaps.

### 1.10 Compulsive summaries

**Looks like:** "In conclusion," "In summary," "Overall," "Ultimately," followed by a restatement of the last three paragraphs. Often appears even in a five-sentence answer.

**Why it reads as AI:** Chat models were trained to wrap up. Human writers end when the point lands.

**Fix:** End on your strongest concrete point. If a piece is long enough to genuinely need a recap, summarize with new compression, not repetition.

### 1.11 The "challenges and future prospects" formula

**Looks like:** "Despite its success, the company faces challenges..." followed by a paragraph of generic obstacles and a hopeful "Looking ahead..." close. In articles, dedicated sections titled Challenges, Future Prospects, or Legacy filled with statements that could apply to any subject.

**Why it reads as AI:** It is the model's default essay skeleton for any topic: intro, praise, obligatory balance, optimistic exit.

**Fix:** Only discuss challenges you can name specifically, with sources or firsthand knowledge. Cut the horoscope-grade future talk entirely.

### 1.12 Formula openers and direct address

**Looks like:** "In the world of...", "In today's fast-paced digital landscape...", "When it comes to...", "Whether you're a seasoned professional or just starting out...", "Have you ever wondered...?", "Let's dive in."

**Why it reads as AI:** These are throat-clearing templates that delay the subject. The "whether you're X or Y" move also fakes audience awareness the writer does not have.

**Fix:** First sentence states the subject or the claim. Start where the interesting part starts.

### 1.13 The vocabulary

Words that spike in AI text far above human baseline. Ban them in decorative use; allow them only in quotes or literal technical contexts.

| Cursed | Reach for instead |
|---|---|
| delve | look at, dig into, examine, or just do it |
| tapestry, landscape, realm (metaphorical) | name the actual field or place |
| vibrant, dynamic | describe what is actually happening there |
| crucial, pivotal, vital | say what breaks without it |
| seamless, robust | say what it survives or how it fails |
| leverage, harness, unlock, unleash, empower | use |
| foster, cultivate | build, run, teach, fund (the real verb) |
| garner | get, win, earn |
| showcase, boast | has, shows |
| underscore, highlight (rhetorical) | delete, or state the point directly |
| testament | evidence, or delete the sentence |
| elevate, transform, revolutionize | say what changed, by how much |
| innovative, cutting-edge, state-of-the-art, game-changer | describe the thing; let the reader judge |
| comprehensive, holistic, multifaceted | list what it actually covers |
| intricate, nuanced | explain one actual nuance |
| journey, navigate (metaphorical) | process, figure out, handle |
| embark | start |
| myriad, plethora | many, or the number |
| ever-evolving, fast-paced, in today's world | delete; adds nothing anywhere |
| meticulous, meticulously | careful, or show the care |

### 1.14 Generic smoothing

**Looks like:** No single banned word, but every sentence is a safe average. Specific facts get rounded into general statements: "utilizes advanced technology to improve outcomes" instead of what the machine does; "received positive feedback" instead of who said what.

**Why it reads as AI:** Statistical text regresses to the mean. The details that make writing trustworthy (numbers, names, dates, mechanisms, sensory observation) are exactly what gets smoothed away.

**Fix:** For every paragraph, ask: what here could only have been written by someone who knows this specific subject? If the answer is nothing, go get details or cut the paragraph.

## Part 2: Style and formatting

### 2.1 Em dash density

**Looks like:** The dash as the default pause, several per paragraph, doing jobs commas and periods do fine.

**Why it reads as AI:** The most infamous tell. Not because dashes are wrong (writers have loved them for centuries) but because models deploy them at a density and uniformity no human sustains. A related tell: hyphens where ranges need en dashes (2019-2024 vs 2019–2024).

**Fix:** Default to commas, periods, or restructuring. Keep a dash only where the sentence genuinely benefits from the interruption, which is rare.

### 2.2 Bold overuse and the "Term: definition" bullet

**Looks like:** **Key terms** bolded mid-sentence like a textbook, and lists where every bullet is "**Concept:** one sentence about the concept."

**Why it reads as AI:** Formatting used as a costume for organization. The pattern is so uniform it functions as a watermark.

**Fix:** Bold almost nothing in prose. If a list of definitions is genuinely needed, vary the construction or write it as prose.

### 2.3 List-itis

**Looks like:** Bullets and numbered lists where flowing sentences belong; three-item bullet groups nested under bolded headers; entire answers with no connected prose at all.

**Why it reads as AI:** Lists are cheap structure. They let a model skip the hard work of connecting ideas, which is where actual thinking shows.

**Fix:** If it reads fine as a sentence, it is a sentence. Save lists for content that is truly enumerable: steps, specs, inventories.

### 2.4 Emoji decoration

**Looks like:** 🚀 in headers, ✅ prefixing every list item, 💡 marking every tip.

**Why it reads as AI:** Decoration standing in for tone. In professional or informative writing it signals template, not personality.

**Fix:** Default to zero. In casual channels where emojis are native (a WhatsApp message, a tweet), use them the way the actual audience does: sparingly and specifically.

### 2.5 Title Case Headings

**Looks like:** "Key Benefits Of The New System" as a heading style throughout.

**Why it reads as AI:** Chat models default to title case; most house styles, including Wikipedia's, use sentence case. Mixed or wrong casing marks unedited paste.

**Fix:** Sentence case unless the destination's style guide says otherwise.

### 2.6 Typographic residue

**Looks like:** Curly quotes and apostrophes ("smart quotes") in environments that use straight ones, inconsistent quote styles within one document, spaced hyphens standing in for dashes.

**Why it reads as AI:** Chatbot output ships with typographic punctuation; pasting it unedited into code, wikis, or plain-text contexts leaves fingerprints.

**Fix:** Match the destination. One style, applied consistently, throughout.

### 2.7 The rigid essay skeleton

**Looks like:** Every piece, regardless of purpose, arrives as: brief intro defining the topic, three balanced body sections with parallel headers, conclusion restating the intro.

**Why it reads as AI:** The five-paragraph essay is the model's comfort structure. Real documents are shaped by their content: a memo front-loads the decision, a tutorial follows the task, a story follows the tension.

**Fix:** Ask what the reader needs first and build the structure around that. Delete any section that exists to complete a pattern rather than to inform.

## Part 3: Chatbot residue

The instant giveaways. Any one of these in a deliverable means it was pasted without a single read-through.

### 3.1 Self-reference and cutoff disclaimers

"As an AI language model...", "as of my last training update...", "I don't have access to real-time data...". Never in any deliverable.

### 3.2 Collaborative chatter and sycophancy

"Certainly! Here's a revised version...", "Great question!", "I hope this helps!", "Would you like me to expand on any section?", "You're absolutely right." These belong to the conversation layer, never to the document. And in the conversation layer, drop the flattery anyway: answer, don't applaud.

### 3.3 Placeholders and phrasal templates

"[Insert company name]", "[Your Name]", "[mention specific achievement]". Fill-in-the-blank scaffolding shipped as product.

### 3.4 Link and citation debris

Fragments like `oaicite`, `turn0search0`, `contentReference`, or `utm_source=chatgpt.com` inside URLs; citations to sources that do not exist or do not say what is claimed; DOIs and ISBNs that resolve to nothing. Verify every reference or remove it. An unverified citation is worse than none.

### 3.5 Refusal and meta leftovers

"I cannot provide...", "Note: this is a fictional example...", or visible remnants of the prompt itself. Read the whole output before it ships.

## Part 4: What humans do that you should too

Removing tells is half the job. The other half:

**Commit.** Human writing has a point of view and risks being wrong. Hedge only where uncertainty is real, then hedge honestly and once.

**Be specific past the point of comfort.** Numbers, names, dates, mechanisms, failures. Specificity is the hardest thing to fake and the fastest thing readers trust.

**Vary the rhythm.** Long sentence, then a short one. Fragments, occasionally. Uniform sentence length is a machine hum; read it aloud and break the meter.

**Have proportions.** Spend words where the substance is and rush past the obvious. Equal-length paragraphs on unequal ideas is a template tell.

**Stop.** When the point lands, end. No recap, no bow, no "hope this helps."

## Part 5: Rewrite drills

**Puffery + significance tail**
Before: "The annual hackathon stands as a testament to the community's innovative spirit, fostering collaboration and underscoring the institute's pivotal role in the tech landscape."
After: "About 300 students built 62 projects at this year's hackathon. Four teams got incubator offers."

**Negative parallelism + vocabulary**
Before: "This isn't just another course. It's a transformative journey that empowers you to unlock your potential in the ever-evolving world of AI."
After: "Twelve weeks. You ship four AI products, and the final one goes in front of real users."

**False range + vague attribution**
Before: "From startups to Fortune 500s, experts agree the platform is a game-changer."
After: "Zerodha and two YC startups run their support queues on it. Their published case studies report response times cut roughly in half."

**List-itis + bold overuse**
Before: "**Speed:** The tool is fast. **Flexibility:** The tool is flexible. **Reliability:** The tool is reliable."
After: "It renders a 4K frame in under two seconds, takes plugins in three languages, and has not dropped a job in eight months of daily use."

**Compulsive summary + sycophancy**
Before: "In conclusion, these strategies will help you elevate your content. I hope this helps! Let me know if you'd like me to delve deeper."
After: (delete all of it; the piece ends on the last real point)

## Part 6: Honest caveats

Carry these so the skill stays calibrated rather than paranoid:

None of these signs proves authorship. Models trained on human writing echo human habits, and heavy AI users are drifting toward AI phrasing in their own speech. Wikipedia's own guidance stresses the list is descriptive, not a set of laws, and studies suggest even expert readers misjudge roughly one text in ten. So when editing someone's writing, flag patterns without accusing people. And when writing, remember the standard is not "undetectable." It is: every sentence earns its place, says something specific, and sounds like a person who means it.
